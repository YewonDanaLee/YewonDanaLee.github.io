Industry–Academia Collaboration Project

Long-Term Retail Petroleum Demand Forecasting & Service Station Network Strategy

Growth Hackers × S-OIL  |  2025

Project Background & Objectives

S-OIL, one of Korea's major integrated energy companies, faced a structural challenge: the spread of EVs and hybrid vehicles, combined with demographic shifts and macroeconomic changes, is expected to erode domestic retail petroleum demand over the medium to long term. This raised an urgent strategic question — how many service stations (S/S) will remain viable, where, and what positioning strategy should S-OIL pursue to remain competitive through 2050.

The project was structured around three interdependent analytical tracks:

  1. Retail petroleum demand forecasting — scenario-based, fuel-type-level demand projections through 2050 (gasoline, diesel, kerosene)
  2. Service station count estimation — region-level S/S count projections through 2050, incorporating demand forecasts and local characteristics
  3. S/S network strategy — identifying strategically viable entry and focus regions, proposing a network fitness metric, and deriving prioritized regional S/S investment recommendations

Theme 1. Long-Term Retail Petroleum Demand Forecasting

1-1. Research & Model Selection

Prior to modeling, conducted a systematic review of existing long-term demand forecasting approaches across two categories:

Statistical and economic time-series models:

Reviewed ARIMAX-based multivariate time-series forecasting (applied in Korean health insurance expenditure projections by KIHASA) and Transformer-based AI models for port throughput forecasting (KAIST). Concluded that AI-based deep learning models were unsuitable for this task — their reliance on historical data patterns makes them structurally ill-equipped for long-horizon forecasting where structural regime changes (EV transition, policy inflection) are the key drivers, and their low interpretability is incompatible with scenario-based strategic planning.

National agency energy models:

Reviewed KEEI's EGMS, the US EIA's WEPS+/NEMS, and Cambridge's E3ME (including the FTT:Transport submodule), which model vehicle type transitions, consumer behavior, social influence, and technology cost dynamics. These large-scale models are not reproducible externally, but their transport submodules provided methodological grounding for the vehicle-fleet-based demand decomposition approach adopted here.

Model selection rationale: The final approach uses a hybrid of economic/statistical models that (1) can produce narrow-scope forecasts for retail petroleum specifically, (2) are externally reproducible and interpretable, and (3) enable scenario branching. AI-based models and single-model time-series approaches were explicitly rejected on these grounds.
1-2. Policy Scenario Design

The Korean government's 2030 NDC targets a 40% reduction in greenhouse gas emissions from 2018 levels. Analyzing the actual annual reduction rate from 2018 to 2022 (approximately 1.9% per year), the projected NDC achievement rate under current policy continuity is approximately 60% — implying the NDC target would be reached around 2035 rather than 2030, and full carbon neutrality around 2070 rather than 2050.

Based on this analysis, three scenarios were defined:

  • High scenario: Carbon neutrality achieved in 2065 (accelerated policy)
  • Mid scenario: Carbon neutrality achieved in 2070 (current policy trajectory)
  • Low scenario: Carbon neutrality achieved in 2075 (decelerated policy)

The 5-year interval between scenarios follows the Korean government's "National Carbon Neutrality and Green Growth Basic Plan" update cycle, providing institutional grounding for the scenario spacing.

1-3. Transportation Fuel (Gasoline + Diesel) Demand Forecasting

Decomposition structure: Transportation fuel demand was decomposed into vehicle fleet composition × fuel efficiency, allowing policy scenarios to be translated into fuel demand through vehicle stock projections rather than requiring direct demand-side assumptions.

Gasoline vehicle fleet projection:

  • Hybrid vehicle fleet was projected using a Gompertz S-curve, selected for its saturation property — appropriate for technologies that expand rapidly but eventually plateau. Hybrid vehicle sales share was projected under each scenario reflecting current momentum (34% sales share in Q1 2025) and future EV penetration effects.
  • A hybrid-to-gasoline fuel consumption correction factor was derived empirically: paired comparisons of hybrid vs. corresponding gasoline model fuel efficiency across 10 top-selling hybrid models yielded an average correction factor of approximately 0.7. Statistical validation was performed in two stages: (1) a two-sample t-test confirmed that hybrid and gasoline fuel efficiencies are significantly different (p < 0.0001); (2) a TOST (Two One-Sided Tests) equivalence test confirmed that hybrid efficiency × 0.7 is statistically equivalent to gasoline efficiency (p ≈ 0.046), validating the correction factor.
  • EV penetration projections used a Dynamic Bass Diffusion Model, allowing the innovation coefficient (p) and imitation coefficient (q) to vary over time to accommodate mid-period policy changes that accelerate or decelerate adoption curves.

Diesel vehicle fleet projection:

  • Diesel passenger and commercial vehicle fleets were projected separately given their different regulatory environments. Domestic and international diesel vehicle regulations (Euro 7, domestic equivalent policies) were incorporated as structural constraints.
  • Freight diesel vehicles were treated as a separate category due to their different replacement cycle and higher per-vehicle fuel consumption.

Regression modeling:

After generating vehicle fleet projections for each scenario, OLS regression was used to translate fleet counts into fuel demand:

  • Gasoline demand: R² = 0.8613, MAE = 3,094,447
  • Diesel demand: R² = 0.9734, MAE = 1,950,319 (separate coefficients for passenger/commercial and freight, reflecting ~3× higher per-vehicle consumption of freight vehicles)
1-4. Heating Fuel (Kerosene) Demand Forecasting

Kerosene demand is primarily seasonal (winter heating) driven by heating substitute adoption (city gas, electric heating) rather than vehicle fleet dynamics — requiring a different model structure.

SARIMA was selected to capture both trend and seasonality in monthly kerosene consumption data. The model was validated by backtesting over a historical holdout period and confirmed through residual diagnostics (residuals should be white noise for a well-specified model).

Minimum value correction: Pure SARIMA extrapolation would eventually project demand approaching zero, which is structurally implausible. A floor derived from structural analysis of non-substitutable kerosene use cases was applied, producing bounded projections that converge to a non-zero minimum.

Theme 2. Long-Term Service Station Count Estimation

2-1. Deep Learning-Based S/S Count Projection

A region-level deep learning model was built to project S/S count trajectories per province/metropolitan area (광역시도) through 2050. Deep learning was selected over statistical models specifically because of the need to capture nonlinear interactions between high-dimensional variables (population, vehicle registrations, road length, urbanized area, industrial composition, adjacent region spillovers) that vary substantially across regions.

The model incorporated historical S/S count trends per region, fuel demand projections from Theme 1, and regional structural variables as covariates.

Three regional decay curve typologies were identified:

  • Metropolitan areas (특별시/광역시): Rapid early decline (through ~2035) as high land costs accelerate closures, followed by a slower decline as residual demand stabilizes
  • General provinces (시/도): Gradual early decline, with acceleration in the later period as EV penetration reaches critical mass
  • Special regions (Jeju, Sejong): Idiosyncratic trajectories dominated by region-specific factors (tourism demand, government policy, population plan vs. actual)
2-2. AI-Based Regional Clustering

Two complementary clustering approaches were applied to group regions by future S/S business viability:

k-Means Clustering with PCA:

Five macroeconomic and demographic variables were collected per city/county (population, GRDP, road length, urbanized area, vehicle registrations). PCA was applied for dimensionality reduction before clustering, with feature weights set proportional to each variable's explanatory power for S/S count.

GCN (Graph Convolutional Network) Clustering:

Cities and counties were represented as nodes in a graph, with edges defined by geographic adjacency and inter-regional traffic flow volume. GCN propagates node features through the graph structure, allowing each node's representation to incorporate information from neighboring nodes — capturing spatial spillover effects that non-graph models cannot represent.

Cluster validation through non-obvious pairings:

  • Gwacheon (경기) & Ulleungdo (경북): Both have constrained S/S counts driven by geographic confinement (greenbelt and island constraints respectively), low road length, and limited fleet size
  • Dongducheon (경기) & Wando (전남): A declining satellite city and a rural coastal county cluster together through shared urban-flight/aging dynamics
  • Seosan (충남) & Gangneung (강원): Industrial and tourism cities with similar population and road length despite very different industry bases
2-3. Four-Quadrant Strategic Typology

Regions were classified on a 2D matrix of (S-OIL S/S market share within region) × (projected S/S count decline rate through 2050), producing four strategic quadrants:

QuadrantMarket ShareDecline RateStrategic Implication
Q1HighLowMaintain — well-positioned in resilient markets
Q2HighHighRisk — high exposure in shrinking markets
Q3LowHighAccept — already low presence, let market contract
Q4LowLowEntry candidate — resilient market with room to grow

This framework was evaluated at two time horizons (2035, 2050) to distinguish near-term from long-term strategic priorities, as some regions shift quadrants between the two periods.

Theme 3. S/S Network Strategy

3-1. Entry Candidate Region Selection

Regions classified as Q4 (low S-OIL share, low projected decline) were further evaluated on regional fundamentals to identify priority entry targets. Key criteria: per-S/S petroleum sales volume, GRDP trajectory, population projections, industrial anchor presence, and geographic/logistics positioning.

Priority entry regions identified:

  • Daejeon Metropolitan City: Geographic hub (convergence of Gyeongbu and Honam expressways, KTX/SRT), anchor for advanced manufacturing and R&D (ABCD+QR national initiatives, nano-semiconductor industrial complex)
  • Chungcheongbuk-do / Chungcheongnam-do: High intra-regional disparity — top-5 cities by per-S/S sales volume (Cheongju, Eumseong, Cheonan, Asan) show volumes comparable to Daejeon despite provincial averages appearing mediocre; these specific cities have GRDP growth, logistics access, and proximity to the Seoul metro area
  • Gyeonggi-do: High fixed petroleum demand driven by satellite city growth and large-scale industrial/logistics anchors (Pangyo Techno Valley, Hwaseong industrial parks). Focus cities: Goyang, Suwon, Yongin, Hanam; de-prioritize Yeoncheon, Pocheon, Dongducheon, Yeoju (high S/S density, low per-S/S sales)
3-2. Non-Entry Focus Regions
  • Seoul (Q3): High land costs produce near-term S/S decline, but absolute fuel demand remains large (logistics, taxi, delivery concentration); long-run residual demand justifies maintaining presence
  • Incheon (Q3): New city development (Songdo, Geomdan) drawing population from Seoul, producing faster absolute demand growth; recommended as near-term focus through 2035
  • Sejong (Q3): S/S projection reflects current below-target population (392,000 vs. 2030 government target of 800,000); strategy remains contingent on National Assembly relocation and presidential office policy
  • Gangwon (Q4): Low decline rate reflects geographic constraints (mountainous terrain, scattered communities) rather than demand resilience; per-S/S sales volume is low — flagged as low-priority despite Q4 classification
3-3. Network Fitness Metric

A risk-adjusted sales metric was proposed to provide a quantitative criterion for evaluating network optimization decisions:

Network Fitness = Σ (S-OIL S/S sales per location × regional S/S survival rate)

This metric weights each station's revenue contribution by the probability that stations in its region survive to a future date, operationalizing the concept that a high-revenue station in a rapidly contracting market is less strategically valuable than a comparable station in a stable market. The survival rate is derived directly from the regional S/S count projections produced in Theme 2.
