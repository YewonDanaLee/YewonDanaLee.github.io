Industry–Academia Collaboration Project

ADME-based Drug-likeness (DCC) Prediction

Aigendrug  |  2025

Project Background & Objectives

The goal of this project was to develop an AI algorithm that predicts ADME (Absorption, Distribution, Metabolism, Excretion) properties of novel compounds without direct experimentation, subsequently evaluating their potential drug-likeness.

Traditional drug discovery pipelines rely on costly and time-consuming wet-lab ADME experiments to filter candidate compounds before clinical trials. This project aimed to replace or augment that screening step with a machine learning pipeline that learns ADME-aware molecular representations and transfers that knowledge to a downstream Drug Compound Classification (DCC) task.

Theme 1. Approach & Architecture

1-1. Molecular Representation
  • Input: Chemical compounds were converted into Canonical SMILES strings as the standard molecular representation
  • Graph representation: SMILES were parsed into molecular graphs using RDKit and PyG — atoms as nodes, bonds as edges — enabling GNN-based feature learning over molecular topology
  • Fingerprint representation: Morgan fingerprints were also generated as an alternative fixed-length representation for MLP-based models, capturing local circular substructure patterns
1-2. Multi-Task Learning Pipeline

A two-stage learning process was designed to maximize feature utility across ADME properties and downstream classification:

  1. Encoder Training: The molecular encoder was pre-trained on ADME-specific property prediction tasks (individual absorption, distribution, metabolism, and excretion targets), learning embeddings that capture pharmacokinetically relevant structural features
  2. Drug Compound Classification (DCC): The ADME-aware encoder was transferred to a classification head and fine-tuned on the binary DCC task (drug-like vs. non-drug-like), leveraging the learned ADME representations to improve generalization
The key hypothesis was that ADME prediction quality and DCC accuracy are positively correlated — compounds with better-characterized pharmacokinetics should be more reliably classified for drug-likeness. The multi-task setup was designed to test and exploit this relationship.

Theme 2. Performance Analysis

The proposed AME (MLP-based) model significantly outperformed the baseline approach in predicting drug-likeness probability across all evaluation metrics.

Model Accuracy Precision Recall F1-Score AUROC
AME (Ours) 0.953 0.967 0.937 0.952 0.994
Baseline 0.916 0.927 0.903 0.915 0.976

Key findings:

  • Multi-task learning improved performance across all DCC metrics compared to the single-task baseline
  • A positive correlation was confirmed between ADME prediction quality and final DCC classification accuracy, validating the core design hypothesis
  • AUROC of 0.994 indicates near-perfect rank ordering of drug-like vs. non-drug-like compounds

Theme 3. Validation & Demo Cases

PCA-based visualization was applied to the learned molecular embeddings to verify that the model's internal representation space positions drug-like and non-drug-like compounds in meaningfully separated regions. New compounds were projected into this space to confirm that their embedding positions relative to known drugs and non-drugs were consistent with their predicted drug-likeness probabilities.

Case Studies
  • Acetaminophen: Correctly classified as drug-like with high probability — a widely used analgesic with well-characterized ADME properties, serving as a positive control
  • Tetrodotoxin: Correctly identified as non-drug-like (toxin) — a potent neurotoxin with unfavorable safety profile despite high bioactivity, confirming that the model distinguishes pharmacological utility from mere bioactivity
Successfully established an end-to-end pipeline for molecular property prediction. Validated that transferring ADME-specific knowledge enhances general drug-likeness classification, building a scalable foundation for virtual screening of novel compounds.
